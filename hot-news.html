Operation Epic Fury - A New WAR!?

In the early hours of Saturday morning, the US and Israel launched air strikes against Iran, kicking off their second war in as many years. In a speech released a few hours later, US President Donald Trump claimed that the strikes were necessary to preempt Iran's attempts to rebuild its nuclear weapons program, although he claimed that it had been obliterated by the US just last year, as well as wanting to take out its long-range missile program, which could soon reach the American homeland.

“And we sought repeatedly to make a deal. We tried. They wanted to do it. They didn't want to do it again. Then again, they wanted to do it. They didn't want to do it. They didn't know what was happening. They just wanted to practice evil”, were the lines said in Trump’s speech.

Trump also signalled that even though the first wave of strikes killed Iran's Supreme Leader, this was not going to be a quick operation, as we saw in Venezuela. He talked about a massive and ongoing operation and warned that American lives might be lost in the process.

“My administration has taken every possible step to minimise the risk to US personnel in the region. Even so, and I do not make this statement lightly, the Iranian regime seeks to kill. The lives of courageous American heroes may be lost, and we may have casualties. That often happens in war. But we're doing this not for now. We're doing this for the future. And it is a noble mission”, were, once again, the words in Trump’s speech.

Nonetheless, Trump did not suggest that American boots would be on the ground and instead called on the Iranian people to rise and effect regime change.

“Finally, to the great proud people of Iran, I say tonight that the hour of your freedom is at hand. Stay sheltered. Don't leave your home. It's very dangerous outside. Bombs will be dropping everywhere. When we are finished, take over your government. It will be yours to take. This will probably be your only chance for generations. For many years, you have asked for America's help, but you never got it. No president was willing to do what I am willing to do tonight. Now, you have a president who is giving you what you want. So, let's see how you respond”, Trump said to the Iranians.

What Caused the War?

So, while Trump obviously listed a litany of allegations going all the way back to the 70s in his speech, the approximate cause of this war seems to have been a breakdown in negotiations between Iran and the US.

According to reports, there seemed to have been two major stumbling blocks in these negotiations.

Firstly, the US wanted to expand the scope of negotiations beyond just Iran's nuclear program to include two other topics, namely Iran's ballistic missile program. With the US pushing for limits on both the size and range of Iran's missiles, as well as Iran's proxy network, the US is pushing for Iran to halt all support to its regional proxies like Hezbollah and Hamas.

Again, according to reports, Iran basically refused to expand the scope of negotiations and instead insisted that the nuclear file be treated in isolation.

But the second stumbling block was that even on the topic of the nuclear program, Iran and the US were unable to come to a deal due to a persistent disagreement over the question of uranium enrichment.

For context, uranium needs to be enriched slightly before it can be used as a fuel in nuclear reactors. But if it's continually enriched, it can then be used in nuclear warheads.

The US insisted that Iran could not have any domestic enrichment capabilities because this would give Iran the means to make a warhead. But Iran signaled that while it would accept limits on enrichment verified by international observers, it needed at least some enrichment capabilities both for reasons of energy independence and because Iran believes that it's entitled to domestic enrichment capabilities under the nuclear non-proliferation treaty to which Iran is a signatory and which guarantees non-nuclear states the right to the production and use of nuclear energy for peaceful purposes without discrimination via Article 4.

A Strategic Misunderstanding

More broadly, though, there seems to have been what I might call a strategic misunderstanding between the two sides.

Trump basically seems to have assumed that because the US is a more powerful country, and because Iran didn't fare too well in the 12-day war last year, Iran would just accept his demands.

Trump's Middle East envoy Steve Witkoff made this clear in an interview with Fox News last month, where, as the US was amassing forces around Iran, he said that Trump was curious as to why they hadn't capitulated.

“I know that we have red lines here from the US. How about over with Iran?” said the reporter. Witkoff replies, “The president asked me that this morning, and he's… I don't want to use the word frustrated. It's almost because he understands he's got plenty of alternatives, but it's curious. He's curious as to why they haven't. I don't want to use the word capitulated, but why haven't they capitulated? Why, under this sort of pressure, with the amount of sea power, naval power that we have over there, why haven't they come to us and said, ‘We profess that we don't want to be, we don't want a weapon. So, here's what we're prepared to do.’ And yet, it's hard to sort of get them to that place.”

But while it's obviously true that the US is a lot more militarily powerful than Iran, the Iranian regime apparently believed that it couldn't afford to make concessions during these negotiations because this would just encourage the US, which is always going to be more powerful, to come back and extract more concessions later.

This is why, in the same way that the smaller guy in prison always has to put up a fight against the bigger guy, the Iranian regime apparently decided that it had to put up some sort of resistance in the face of America's military might to reestablish a degree of deterrence.

You can think of this in game theory terms. Trump was apparently framing negotiations as a single game, in which case it would have been rational for Iran to accept whatever concessions it needed to avoid being attacked.

But instead, the Iranian regime is thinking about this as an iterative game, in which case it's rational to put up a fight to impose at least some costs on Trump to deter him from further aggression in the future.

Game theory also explains why war became pretty much inevitable once the US had amassed its massive military presence around Iran. Given that any US-Israeli attack was going to focus on Iran's missile systems, this created a use-it-or-lose-it incentive for Iran to strike first and consequently an incentive for the US and Israel to strike early in anticipation.

The Strike and Immediate Aftermath

The specific timing of Saturday's strike seems to have been driven by intelligence obtained by the US and Israel about a meeting of Iran's senior leadership at Supreme Leader Ayatollah Ali Khamenei’s compound in Tehran.

The compound itself was obliterated, killing the Ayatollah and a number of senior officials, including his chief adviser and the head of the Iranian Revolutionary Guard Corps.

In response, Iran launched missile attacks at not just Israeli and US forces in the region, but also at non-military targets in nearby Arab countries, including oil facilities in Saudi Arabia and hotels in the UAE.

Since then, Iran and Israel have continued to trade missile strikes, with a fresh wave of strikes hitting Tehran on Sunday morning.

What Happens Next?

Broadly speaking, I see three possible outcomes in Iran.

First is the most optimistic one: that Khamenei’s death paves the way for some sort of positive regime change, ending with a government that's more moderate and more popular inside Iran.

The second is that despite being decapitated, the regime hangs on in one form or another. Unlike Venezuela, the Iranian regime is not a personalistic dictatorship and is more institutionalised than many assume. If it survives, Khamenei will likely be succeeded by someone more hardline, potentially doubling down on the nuclear program.

The third possibility is that Iran collapses into some sort of civil war with dire consequences for the Iranian people and the wider region.

Syria is a useful benchmark here. In 2011, when the Syrian civil war began, Syria had a population of about 20 million people. Iran today has a population of 92 million people and significantly more dangerous weaponry, meaning that an Iranian civil war could be far more destabilising than Syria’s was.
